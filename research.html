MSc Thesis · The American University in Cairo · 2023

Mixed-Criticality Scheduling Using Reinforcement Learning

Supervised by Dr. Nouri Sakr · Master of Science in Computer Science

The problem

Mixed-criticality systems run jobs of different safety importance on shared hardware — the setup behind automotive and avionics safety standards like ISO 26262 and DO-178B. The hard version of this problem is online: the scheduler only knows about jobs that have already arrived, the processor's speed can degrade unpredictably, and once a job starts running it can't be paused. That combination is provably NP-hard, and prior work had mostly solved easier versions — offline, preemptive, or with simplified degradation models.

My approach

I tackled the online, non-preemptive, varying-speed version directly, using deep reinforcement learning instead of classical scheduling policies. I built a custom Gym environment and reward function, then trained and compared four algorithms — a custom Ape-X DQN, plain DQN, PPO, and Augmented Random Search — across thousands of generated job instances with randomized load, criticality mix, and degradation risk.

RL algorithms benchmarked

The custom Ape-X agent scheduled sets of 50 jobs correctly 95.1% of the time on average, staying above 90% even under the heaviest, most degradation-prone workloads — without ever seeing the full future job sequence in advance. It outperformed standard DQN (93%), Augmented Random Search (83%), and PPO (79%) on the same benchmarks. A preemptive variant of the same agent pushed average completion to 96%.

To the best of my knowledge, this is the first reinforcement-learning scheduler evaluated on the online, non-preemptive, varying-speed mixed-criticality problem.
